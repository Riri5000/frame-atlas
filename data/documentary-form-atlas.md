# Documentary Form Atlas

*A living reference for categorizing documentary styles and approaches. Built across multiple research sessions. Each axis is independent: a single film can be novel on one axis and conventional on another.*

Source of truth: Notion page "Documentary Form Atlas" plus the three axis sub-pages (axes 3, 4, 5), exported 2026-07-20. This file is the seed input for `scripts/seed-taxonomy.ts`.

Films marked **★ watch first** are the clearest demonstration of their category.

---

# Axis 1: Filming technique as the story

*Films where the method of capture is not just a stylistic choice but is inseparable from, or literally is, the story.*

## 1.1 Archival-only (no new footage shot)

The director never picks up a camera. The craft is entirely in research, rights, and editing. Interviews, if any, are audio-only laid over archive.

- **Senna** (2010, Asif Kapadia) ★ watch first. Defined the modern archival feature: no talking heads, present-tense storytelling built purely from race footage, home video, and broadcast archive. Kapadia repeated the formula with **Amy** (2015) and **Diego Maradona** (2019).
- **Apollo 11** (2019, Todd Douglas Miller). Assembled from rediscovered 65mm NASA archive and 11,000 hours of audio. No narration, no interviews, no reenactment. The archive quality is the spectacle.
- **They Shall Not Grow Old** (2018, Peter Jackson). Restoration as technique: WWI footage retimed, colorized, with forensic lip-readers reconstructing dialogue. Controversial and worth studying for the ethics of enhancement.
- **Summer of Soul** (2021, Questlove). Built around a single unseen archive (1969 Harlem Cultural Festival tapes) with present-day reaction interviews woven in. The rediscovery is part of the narrative.
- **The Velvet Underground** (2021, Todd Haynes). Archival plus persistent split-screen borrowed from Warhol's Exploding Plastic Inevitable, so the form of the film mimics the aesthetic of its subject.

## 1.2 Animation as documentary

Animation used not as decoration but because the events could not be filmed, or to protect identity, or to represent memory and trauma more honestly than reenactment.

- **Waltz with Bashir** (2008, Ari Folman) ★ watch first. Animated interviews about the 1982 Lebanon war. Animation represents the unreliability of traumatic memory; the switch to real archive footage in the final seconds is the whole argument of the film.
- **Flee** (2021, Jonas Poher Rasmussen). Animation protects the anonymity of an Afghan refugee while allowing full emotional intimacy. First film nominated for Oscar best documentary, animated feature, and international feature simultaneously.
- **Tower** (2016, Keith Maitland). Rotoscoped reenactments of the 1966 UT Austin shooting, blended with archive. Rotoscope lets survivors appear as their younger selves.
- **Fire of Love** (2022, Sara Dosa). Primarily archival (the Kraffts' own volcano footage) but uses paper-cutout animation for gaps, a good study in mixing 1.1 and 1.2.

## 1.3 Cameras given to subjects, or subjects as cinematographers

The power of authorship shifts. Directly relevant to participatory video methodology.

- **Midnight Traveler** (2019, Hassan Fazili) ★ watch first. An Afghan family's three-year refugee journey filmed entirely on three mobile phones by the family themselves. The constraint is total and the intimacy is unmatched.
- **For Sama** (2019, Waad al-Kateab). Five years of self-shot footage in besieged Aleppo, framed as a letter to the filmmaker's daughter. First-person capture plus epistolary structure.
- **20 Days in Mariupol** (2023, Mstyslav Chernov). A news journalist's own footage recut as cinema; the story includes the mechanics of getting the footage out.
- **Born into Brothels** (2004, Zana Briski). Children in Calcutta's red-light district given still cameras; their photographs become both the method and a plot engine. The canonical participatory-media example, also much critiqued, worth reading the criticism.
- **The Territory** (2022, Alex Pritz). Mid-production, the Uru-eu-wau-wau community took over their own cinematography during COVID and received co-production credit. A modern model for shared authorship.

## 1.4 Non-human and rig-based cameras

The camera is unmoored from a human eye, producing perspectives no operator could hold.

- **Leviathan** (2012, Lucien Castaing-Taylor and Verena Paravel) ★ watch first. Dozens of GoPros attached to a fishing trawler, to fishermen, tossed into fish bins and underwater. From Harvard's Sensory Ethnography Lab. No interviews, no context, pure sensation. This film launched a whole school.
- **Gunda** (2020, Viktor Kossakovsky). Black and white, animal-height cameras, no music, no narration, no humans. The technique forces you to watch a pig as a protagonist.
- **Cow** (2021, Andrea Arnold). Similar commitment applied to a single dairy cow over years, but with a harsher industrial framing.
- **Chasing Ice** (2012, Jeff Orlowski). Time-lapse rigs left on glaciers for years. The building and maintaining of the camera systems IS the narrative spine, and the resulting images are the evidence.

## 1.5 Screen-based, remote, and telepresence capture

The film is made of screens: livestreams, video calls, desktop footage. Increasingly important post-2020.

- **Present.Perfect.** (2019, Shengze Zhu). Assembled entirely from Chinese livestream broadcasts. The director never filmed anything; she watched and curated hundreds of hours of streams.
- **Put Your Soul on Your Hand and Walk** (2025, Sepideh Farsi) ★ watch first. Constructed from a year of video calls between the director and Gaza photojournalist Fatma Hassona. The low-res call footage is the point: distance, fragility of connection, and what a phone link can and cannot carry.
- **Transformers: The Premake** (2014, Kevin B. Lee). A "desktop documentary" made inside a computer screen, analyzing a blockbuster through YouTube clips, maps, and browser windows. Short and free online, foundational for the desktop-doc form.

## 1.6 Technique as protection: face-swaps, deepfakes, voice synthesis

Digital disguise and synthesis as both tool and subject.

- **Welcome to Chechnya** (2020, David France) ★ watch first. AI face-swapping ("digital veils") protects LGBTQ+ activists while preserving their real facial emotion. The moment a subject "removes" the veil on screen is a formal event in the story.
- **Another Body** (2023, Sophie Compton and Reuben Hamlyn). A deepfake-abuse victim is herself protected by a consensual deepfake face. The technique mirrors the subject perfectly.
- **Roadrunner** (2021, Morgan Neville). Used AI-synthesized Anthony Bourdain voice for a few lines without disclosure. Study it as the cautionary ethics case.

## 1.7 Specialized optics: 3D, IMAX, forensic imaging

- **Cave of Forgotten Dreams** (2010, Werner Herzog). 3D justified by subject: the Chauvet cave paintings were made on curved rock, and depth is essential to seeing them as their makers did.
- **Pina** (2011, Wim Wenders). 3D for dance, capturing choreography's spatial dimension that flat film loses.
- **2000 Meters to Andriivka** (2025, Mstyslav Chernov). Ukrainian assault filmed largely through soldiers' bodycams and drone feeds, stitched into near-continuous forward movement. Operational military footage as first-person cinema.

## 1.8 Generative and variable-form documentary

- **Eno** (2024, Gary Hustwit). Software generates a different cut of the Brian Eno documentary at every screening; no two audiences see the same film. Form matches Eno's generative music philosophy. Currently the defining example of this category.

# Axis 2: Interview approach

*How testimony is extracted, staged, performed, withheld, or replaced. The interview is the most conventional tool in documentary, which makes deviations from it the most legible innovations.*

## 2.1 The engineered gaze

Hardware built to change what an interview feels like on screen.

- **The Fog of War** (2003, Errol Morris) ★ watch first. The Interrotron: a modified teleprompter rig that lets the subject look directly into the lens while seeing Morris' face. Direct eye contact with the audience, impossible in conventional off-axis interviews. Also see **The Thin Blue Line** (1988), where stylized reenactments plus interviews literally overturned a murder conviction.
- **Beckham** (2023, Fisher Stevens). A replay screen placed just beside the lens shows subjects footage of decisive moments while a tight lens captures their unguarded reaction. The interview becomes about watching someone remember.
- **Look into My Eyes** (2024, Lana Wilson). Psychic readings filmed as intimate two-person sessions in natural light, with no captions, narration, or music. The "interview" is a session between subjects, not between subject and filmmaker.

## 2.2 Testimony as the entire film

No archive, no b-roll crutch. The act of remembering on camera is the film.

- **Shoah** (1985, Claude Lanzmann) ★ watch first. Nine and a half hours, zero archival footage. Only present-day testimony and the landscapes where the Holocaust happened. Lanzmann also used a hidden camera (the "paluche") to record perpetrators who refused to be filmed, and shows the surveillance van in the film. The ethical foundation stone for the whole axis.
- **The Look of Silence** (2014, Joshua Oppenheimer). An optometrist whose brother was murdered in the 1965 Indonesian genocide interviews the killers while fitting them for glasses. The eye-test frame gives the confrontation a devastating formal logic.

## 2.3 Reenactment as interview

Subjects don't describe events; they perform them, and the performance is the testimony.

- **The Act of Killing** (2012, Joshua Oppenheimer) ★ watch first. Death squad leaders restage their killings in the film genres of their choice (gangster, western, musical). The gap between their self-image and their acts becomes visible on camera. Arguably the most influential formal invention in 21st century documentary.
- **Procession** (2021, Robert Greene). Six survivors of clergy abuse write and direct their own reenactment scenes, with a drama therapist involved throughout. Direction as therapy, therapy as film.
- **Casting JonBenet** (2017, Kitty Green). Local actors from the Ramseys' hometown audition for roles in a film that never gets made. Audition-room monologues expose a community's theories, projections, and private griefs. The casting session replaces the interview entirely.
- **Bisbee '17** (2018, Robert Greene) *(cross-listed from watchlist)*. A whole town restages a 1917 mass deportation. Civic-scale reenactment as collective interview.

## 2.4 Verbatim performance

Real interview audio, performed bodies.

- **The Arbor** (2010, Clio Barnard) ★ watch first. Actors lip-sync precisely to real recorded interviews about playwright Andrea Dunbar. The visible seam between real voice and performing body keeps you constantly aware of mediation. A one-film category that many have imitated and none matched.

## 2.5 No interview at all

The deliberate refusal, forcing observation to carry everything.

- **Frederick Wiseman: institutional portraits** (1967 to 2023, Frederick Wiseman) ★ watch first: any one Wiseman, e.g. **National Gallery** (2014). From **Titicut Follies** (1967) through **Menus-Plaisirs** (2023): six decades of institutional portraits with no interviews, narration, captions, or music. Structure emerges purely in the edit.
- **Salesman** (1969) and **Grey Gardens** (1975, Maysles brothers). American direct cinema: presence without formal interview, though Grey Gardens lets subjects address the camera conversationally.
- **Honeyland** (2019, Kotevska and Stefanov). Three years of pure observation of a Macedonian beekeeper; narrative arc entirely found, never asked for.

## 2.6 Staged and performative conversation

Conversations engineered or fictionalized to reach truths a Q&A cannot.

- **20,000 Days on Earth** (2014, Forsyth and Pollard) ★ watch first. Nick Cave's "day" is entirely constructed: scripted drives with ghosts from his past appearing in the passenger seat, a session with a psychoanalyst as the interview device. Staged frame, real disclosure.
- **Faces Places** (2017, Agnes Varda and JR). Encounters rather than interviews: the filmmakers' road trip and friendship is the elicitation method. Varda's whole late style (**The Gleaners and I** (2000), **The Beaches of Agnes** (2008)) treats conversation as gleaning.
- **Grizzly Man** (2005, Werner Herzog). Includes the inverse move: Herzog listens on camera to the audio of Timothy Treadwell's death and refuses to let us hear it. The withheld interview as ethical statement.
- **Sr.** (2022, Chris Smith). Robert Downey Jr. interviews his dying father while the father simultaneously edits his own version of the film. Two films arguing with each other.

## 2.7 Interview as investigation and sting

The interview is an active operation; asking questions changes the events.

- **The Jinx** (2015, Andrew Jarecki) ★ watch first. Robert Durst's hot-mic bathroom monologue. The interview setting itself became evidence in a murder trial. Study it alongside the ethics debate about what the filmmakers knew and when.
- **Navalny** (2022, Daniel Roher). Navalny prank-calls his own would-be assassins on camera and extracts a confession in real time. The sting call as centerpiece scene.
- **The Mole** (2020, Mads Brugger) *(cross-listed from watchlist)*. Ten years of hidden-camera infiltration; every conversation is simultaneously an interview and an undercover operation.

## 2.8 Group process instead of interview

- **The Work** (2017, Jairus McLeary and Gethin Aldous) ★ watch first. Four days inside group therapy at Folsom Prison. No sit-down interviews at all; the group session's own emotional mechanics replace every conventional elicitation tool. One of the most intense uses of pure process ever filmed.

# Axis 3: Narrative structure

*How the true story unfolds in time: borrowed genre engines, withheld information, repetition, essayistic drift, and structures that make the film's own construction part of the story.*

## 3.1 Genre engines borrowed from fiction

Nonfiction events poured into a fiction genre's suspense machinery.

- **Man on Wire** (2008, James Marsh) ★ watch first. Philippe Petit's 1974 World Trade Center walk told as a heist movie: the team, the plan, the break-in, the job. Proof that structure alone can transform archive plus interview into cinema.
- **The Imposter** (2012, Bart Layton). A con man narrates his own deception directly to camera, and the film lets him con the audience too before pulling the rug. Unreliable narrator as structural principle, ending deliberately unresolved.
- **Icarus** (2017, Bryan Fogel). Starts as a Super Size Me style self-experiment about doping, then the subject becomes a whistleblower and the film transforms into a geopolitical thriller mid-stream. The gold standard for a documentary that changes genre while you watch.
- **Free Solo** (2018, Chin and Vasarhelyi). Engineering suspense when the outcome is publicly known, partly by making the crew's fear of filming a death part of the story.
- **Searching for Sugar Man** (2012, Malik Bendjelloul). Structured as a detective mystery, withholding a fact known to millions of South Africans to preserve the reveal for everyone else. Worth studying precisely because you know what it hides.
- **Three Identical Strangers** (2018, Tim Wardle). A cascade structure: each act detonates the premise of the previous one, from feel-good reunion to ethics horror story.

## 3.2 Withheld information and the structural gut-punch

The order of revelation is the film's real authorship.

- **Dear Zachary** (2008, Kurt Kuenne) ★ watch first. A memorial film whose mid-film and late-film revelations are structured for maximum devastation. Divisive for exactly that reason: study it as the extreme case of editing as emotional engineering, and form your own view on whether it crosses a line.
- **Capturing the Friedmans** (2003, Andrew Jarecki). Contradictory home movies and testimony arranged so certainty keeps dissolving. The structure refuses to adjudicate.

## 3.3 Repetition and the Rashomon structure

The same event replayed with variations until the replaying becomes the argument.

- **The Thin Blue Line** (1988, Errol Morris) ★ watch first. The roadside shooting reenacted again and again, each version reflecting a different witness's claim. Repetition exposes testimony as construction, and the film freed an innocent man.

## 3.4 The essay film

An authorial voice thinking on screen; association replaces chronology.

- **Sans Soleil** (1983, Chris Marker) ★ watch first. Fictional letters from an invented cameraman, read by an unnamed woman, drifting across Japan, Guinea-Bissau, and memory itself. The headwater of the entire essay tradition.
- **F for Fake** (1973, Orson Welles). An essay about forgery that forges itself: Welles openly promises to tell the truth for one hour, then exploits the expiry of that promise. Editing as sleight of hand.
- **Cameraperson** (2016, Kirsten Johnson). A memoir assembled entirely from outtakes of other people's films the cinematographer shot over 25 years. Structure by resonance rather than argument.
- **13th** (2016, Ava DuVernay). The argumentative essay at full force: a single thesis (the 13th Amendment's loophole) prosecuted across 150 years of evidence.
- **Soundtrack to a Coup d'Etat** (2024, Johan Grimonprez). The Lumumba assassination cut to the rhythms of jazz, with musicians as both soundtrack and historical actors. Montage-as-argument at a density few films attempt.

## 3.5 The making of the film becomes the film

Construction exposed as content.

- **Stories We Tell** (2012, Sarah Polley) ★ watch first. A family secret investigated through competing family narrators, with "archival" Super 8 footage revealed late to be staged reconstruction. The reveal reframes everything you trusted.
- **Close-Up** (1990, Abbas Kiarostami). A real trial of a man who impersonated a filmmaker, with all participants replaying their own roles. The founding text of fiction/nonfiction hybrid structure.
- **Wormwood** (2017, Errol Morris). Interviews interleaved with lavish scripted drama starring Peter Sarsgaard. Docudrama not as reenactment filler but as a co-equal narrative strand.

## 3.6 Epistolary and diary structure

The film is addressed to someone, and the address shapes everything.

- **News from Home** (1976, Chantal Akerman) ★ watch first. Static shots of New York while Akerman reads her mother's increasingly plaintive letters from Belgium. Distance between image and text is the entire emotional content.
- **For Sama** (2019) *(cross-listed from 1.3)*. The letter-to-a-daughter frame turns war footage into testament.
- **Time** (2020, Garrett Bradley). Two decades of a mother's home-video diaries braided non-chronologically with the present, collapsing past and present into one continuous act of waiting.

## 3.7 Non-verbal symphonic structure

No dialogue, no interviews, no text. Structure is musical: movements, motifs, crescendos.

- **Man with a Movie Camera** (1929, Dziga Vertov) ★ watch first. The city symphony and the reflexive documentary invented simultaneously, a century ago, and still formally ahead of most contemporary work.
- **Koyaanisqatsi** (1982, Godfrey Reggio). Image and Philip Glass score in strict partnership; the argument about technology and speed is carried entirely by rhythm.
- **Samsara** (2011, Ron Fricke). The 70mm globe-spanning successor; useful for studying how motif placement substitutes for narrative.

## 3.8 Non-human and impossible narrators

The newest structural frontier: voice given to things that cannot speak.

- **Dahomey** (2024, Mati Diop) ★ watch first. Twenty-six looted royal artifacts return from Paris to Benin, and one of them narrates in a processed, subterranean voice. Golden Bear winner; the impossible narrator reframes restitution from the object's point of view.
- **Aanikoobijigan** (2026, Adam and Zack Khalil). Anishinaabe ancestors trapped in museum archives "bend time and space to find their way home." Premiered at Sundance 2026; a companion piece to Dahomey and possibly the start of a movement.

# Axis 4: Filmmaker-subject relationship

*Who holds power over the story, how long the relationship lasts, and what happens when the line between filmmaker and subject blurs, breaks, or is deliberately erased.*

## 4.1 Longitudinal embedding: the decades-long relationship

Time itself becomes the method. The relationship outlasts marriages, careers, and sometimes the filmmaker.

- **The Up series** (1964 to 2019, Michael Apted) ★ watch first. Fourteen British children revisited every seven years for 56 years. The subjects' evolving resentment and negotiation of the project is increasingly part of the films themselves.
- **Hoop Dreams** (1994, Steve James). Five years with two Chicago basketball hopefuls; the film's honesty about class and dashed promise came from duration no shoot schedule could fake.
- **Quest** (2017, Jonathan Olshefski). Ten years with one North Philadelphia family, begun as a photo essay. The modest, patient model of embedding.
- **The Staircase** (2004 to 2018, Jean-Xavier de Lestrade). Sixteen years following a murder case, complicated by the revelation that the editor fell in love with the subject during production. A landmark ethics case study about proximity.

## 4.2 The filmmaker inside the subject group

No outsider access problem because there is no outside.

- **Minding the Gap** (2018, Bing Liu) ★ watch first. Liu films his own skateboarding friends over twelve years, then turns the camera on his own history of domestic abuse, and interviews his mother about it on camera. The moment the director becomes a subject is the film's hinge.
- **Strong Island** (2017, Yance Ford). The director investigates the killing of his own brother and the justice system's indifference, addressing the camera in extreme close-up. Grief as method.

## 4.3 Collaborative staging with the subject

The subject is a creative partner in constructing scenes about their own life.

- **Dick Johnson Is Dead** (2020, Kirsten Johnson) ★ watch first. Johnson repeatedly stages her father's death and heaven in slapstick fantasy sequences, with his delighted participation, as a way of pre-grieving his dementia together. Collaboration as an act of love.
- **The Missing Picture** (2013, Rithy Panh). Panh reconstructs his family's destruction under the Khmer Rouge using hand-carved clay figurines, because no images exist and none could be trusted. Subject, filmmaker, and survivor are one person.

## 4.4 The performative filmmaker

The director's on-screen persona is the elicitation instrument.

- **Roger & Me** (1989, Michael Moore) ★ watch first. The ambush quest as structure: Moore's failed attempts to meet GM's CEO organize the whole film. Study alongside the criticism of his timeline manipulations.
- **Kurt & Courtney** (1998) and **Biggie & Tupac** (2002, Nick Broomfield). The process of failing to make the film becomes the film: boom mic in shot, phone calls on camera, doors closing.
- **Louis Theroux's Weird Weekends** onward (1998 to present, Louis Theroux). The faux-naive persona that lets subjects reveal themselves by explaining things to an apparently harmless man.
- **Grizzly Man** (2005, Werner Herzog) *(cross-listed from 2.6)*. Herzog argues with his dead subject's worldview in narration, making the film a posthumous debate between two filmmakers.

## 4.5 Shared and transferred authorship

The formal answer to 'who gets to tell this story': give the tools away or share the credit.

- **No Other Land** (2024, Basel Adra, Yuval Abraham, Hamdan Ballal, Rachel Szor) ★ watch first. A Palestinian-Israeli collective co-directs the destruction of Masafer Yatta, with the friendship and asymmetry between Adra and Abraham examined inside the film. Won the 2025 Oscar without a US distributor.
- **The Fogo Process** (1967, Colin Low and the NFB's Challenge for Change). The historical root of participatory video: 27 short films made with Fogo Island fishermen, screened back to the community and to government, with the feedback loop changing policy. Not a single film to watch so much as a methodology to study, and the direct ancestor of the participatory video practice used by RUCE-style community work today.
- **The Territory** (2022) *(cross-listed from 1.3)*. Community takes over cinematography mid-film and earns co-production credit.
- **Born into Brothels** (2004) *(cross-listed from 1.3)*. The cautionary contrast: participation as method but not as authorship, which is exactly what its critics attack.

## 4.6 The subject fights back

Contested authorship: the relationship becomes adversarial and the resistance becomes the story.

- **Tickled** (2016, David Farrier and Dylan Reeve) ★ watch first. What starts as a quirky story about competitive tickling turns into legal threats, private investigators, and the subject actively hunting the filmmakers during production. The film documents its own besiegement.
- **Catfish** (2010, Schulman and Joost). The filmmakers' relationship with their subject inverts when they discover the deception; the confrontation is handled with unexpected tenderness. Also a permanent ethics debate about what they knew and when.

## 4.7 Posthumous and archival relationships

The subject is gone; the relationship is with what they left.

- **Val** (2021, Ting Poo and Leo Scott) ★ watch first. Built from Val Kilmer's decades of self-shot video, narrated by his son in his father's cadence after throat cancer took Kilmer's voice. The archive is the subject's own lifelong self-documentation.
- **Amy** (2015, Asif Kapadia) *(cross-listed from 1.1)*. The archival relationship at its most ethically fraught: friends who supplied footage later objected to the film's framing.

# Axis 5: Format and length constraints

*What duration, platform, and delivery mechanism do to a true story. Constraint is not a limitation on form; it is usually the thing that generates it.*

## 5.1 The curated short (5 to 40 minutes)

Institutionally commissioned shorts with festival-grade craft, built for a single idea held to its natural length.

- **NYT Op-Docs** and **Field of Vision** ★ study first as institutions rather than single films. Both publish free online and represent the two poles of the commissioned short: Op-Docs as an opinion page in film form, Field of Vision (co-founded by Laura Poitras) as urgent political cinema. Watching ten of each is the fastest possible education in what short nonfiction can do.
- **A Love Song for Latasha** (2020, Sophia Nahli Allison, 19 min). A murdered girl remembered through dreamlike reconstruction and the voices of those who loved her, refusing news-story framing entirely. What lyricism can do in under twenty minutes.
- **The Elephant Whisperers** (2022, Kartiki Gonsalves, 41 min). The Netflix-era prestige short: feature-quality cinematography compressed, Oscar winner, and a case study in the 40-minute ceiling.

## 5.2 Vertical and platform-native documentary

The 9:16 frame and sub-3-minute duration as native conditions, not adaptations. Moving fast right now.

- **60 Second Docs** ★ study first. The institutionalized micro-doc: one subject, one minute, hard structural formula (hook, character, turn, button). Hundreds of episodes make the formula legible fast.
- The vertical ecosystem is professionalizing rapidly: TikTok and Sundance Collab launched a micro-series program in June 2026, dedicated vertical platforms and festivals now exist, and YouTube Shorts reportedly averages 200 billion daily views. Mostly colonized by scripted microdrama so far, which means vertical documentary is still comparatively open territory. Directly relevant to the We're Not Doomed format.
- Study also how news orgs (Washington Post, AJ+) structure vertical explainers: captions as primary text layer, first-frame hook, sound-off legibility.

## 5.3 The docuseries and the very long feature

Streaming economics reshaped nonfiction length; the best long works earn their duration.

- **O.J.: Made in America** (2016, Ezra Edelman, 467 min) ★ watch first. Nearly eight hours that uses length to fuse biography, city history, and race in America into one argument. Won the Oscar as a film, after which the Academy banned multi-part works, the rare case where a work forced a rule change.
- **The Last Dance** (2020, Jason Hehir). The access-deal series: Jordan's production company held sway over the archive, making it the key case study in what access costs editorially.
- **The Jinx** (2015) *(cross-listed from 2.7)*. The series format's capacity for a cliffhanger becoming a news event.

## 5.4 Durational extremes and slow TV

Length as ethical and aesthetic position: refusing compression.

- **Tie Xi Qu: West of the Tracks** (2002, Wang Bing, 551 min) ★ watch first (in parts). Nine hours on the death of a Chinese industrial district. Duration is the argument: deindustrialization cannot be understood in ninety minutes. Wang Bing's Youth trilogy (2023 to 2024) continues the practice.
- **NRK Slow TV** (2009 onward). Bergensbanen: seven unbroken hours of a train journey; Hurtigruten: 134 hours of a coastal voyage, live, watched by half of Norway. Broadcast duration as national campfire, and worth knowing about for anything involving a long coastal journey filmed slowly.
- **Shoah** (1985) *(cross-listed from 2.2)*. Nine and a half hours as moral refusal of summary.

## 5.5 Interactive and web-native documentary

The browser as venue. A movement that rose around 2010 to 2015 and largely receded, instructive in both directions.

- **Bear 71** (2012, NFB, Leanne Allison and Jeremy Mendes) ★ experience first. A grizzly tracked by trail cameras, narrated in first person, navigated by the user through a surveillance-grid interface. Still online, still the best argument for the form.
- **Highrise** (2010 to 2015, Katerina Cizek, NFB). Multi-year interactive project on vertical living worldwide; also a landmark of co-creation methodology (overlaps axis 4.5).
- **Welcome to Pine Point** (2011, The Goggles, NFB). A vanished mining town remembered as an interactive scrapbook. The lesson of the whole category: platform dependency killed many of these works, an argument for durable formats.

## 5.6 Immersive: VR and installation

- **Clouds Over Sidra** (2015, Gabo Arora and Chris Milk, for the UN). 360-degree portrait of a Syrian girl in Za'atari camp, screened to donors and credited with measurably increasing pledges. The founding case for VR as an empathy and advocacy format inside the UN system, directly relevant to UN client work.
- **Notes on Blindness: Into Darkness** (2016). Companion VR to the feature, rendering John Hull's audio diaries as spatial sound and dissolving imagery. Form matches subject: what the world is when built from hearing.
- **Carne y Arena** (2017, Alejandro G. Inarritu). Walk-through installation putting one visitor at a time inside a border crossing. Received a special Oscar; the museum piece as documentary venue.

## 5.7 Crowdsourced and event-based capture

The format is an open call; authorship is curatorial.

- **Life in a Day** (2011 and 2021, Kevin Macdonald) ★ watch first. Assembled from 80,000 YouTube submissions filmed on a single day worldwide. The edit finds a day-shaped arc in the planet's home footage. Repeated a decade later as a deliberate time-capsule pair.
- **Eno** (2024) *(cross-listed from 1.8)*. The generative screening as live event: format constraint (every screening unique) becomes the distribution strategy.

# Unclassified watchlist

*Films that resist the axes above. Watch, then categorize or create a new category.*

- **Symbiopsychotaxiplasm: Take One** (1968, William Greaves). A film about its own making, layered crews filming each other. Possibly its own category of recursive documentary.
- **The Mole** (2020, Mads Brugger). Ten-year undercover infiltration of North Korean arms dealing by a civilian. Journalism, prank, and thriller at once. *(Now also placed in 2.7.)*
- **All Light, Everywhere** (2021, Theo Anthony). Essay on cameras, surveillance, and the blind spot in vision itself. Technique and subject collapse into each other.
- **Austerlitz** (2016, Sergei Loznitsa). Fixed cameras silently watching tourists at concentration camp memorials. Durational observation as moral question.
- **Bisbee '17** (2018, Robert Greene). An entire town restages a 1917 mass deportation of miners. Community reenactment at civic scale. *(Now also placed in 2.3.)*
- **Militantropos** (2025, Tabor Collective). Ukraine war doc noted at Cannes for inventive cinematography. Needs watching to place.
